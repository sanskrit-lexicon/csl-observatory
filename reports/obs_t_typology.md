# Error typology of digital Sanskrit dictionaries (OBS-T)

_Generated by `scripts/obs_t_typology.py` from `correction_events_final.csv` (the unified, component-attributed correction-event table). Sanskrit spans are normalized to IAST where available; raw source/markup edits keep their `edit_space` audit marker. Built offline from the correction-form export, the `../csl-orig` git history, and sibling `../CORRECTIONS` files. Every event carries an evidence label (`observed`/`derived`/`inferred`); entry counts for density come from `../csl-orig` `<L>` markers._

> **Claim (two axes).** Across twelve years and 43 dictionaries, each correction is described on two orthogonal axes: a **location** — which microstructure component it repairs (headword, sense, citation, markup, …) — and an **edit-type** — what kind of change it is (spelling, diacritic, case, spacing, source-raw, …). The largest derived location is **sense**; markup, headword, and citation form the next tier. Minor-edit rates vary sharply by location, so surface-edit claims are reported by location rather than asserted globally. The location profile differs by dictionary and shifts over time.

Complements [`obs_q_correction_sustainability.md`](obs_q_correction_sustainability.md) (who corrects, how fast) by measuring **what** was wrong and **where** in the entry. See [`obs_t_rigor.md`](obs_t_rigor.md) for the tested claims.

## Draft abstract

> We present a twelve-year, 52,498-event corpus of corrections to the Cologne Digital Sanskrit Lexicon (43 dictionaries) and a two-axis typology of the errors they repair. Unifying a 2014–2019 correction-form archive with the 2019–2026 source git history, we normalize Sanskrit spans to IAST (handling the form's mixed Devanagari/Harvard-Kyoto encoding) while retaining raw source strings for audit, and describe each event by its **location** in the dictionary microstructure and its **edit type**. The largest derived location is sense, followed by markup, headword, and citation; minor-edit rates vary sharply by location; the location profile differs markedly by dictionary; and a stable character-confusion signal (led by b/v in the form layer) emerges. We release the corpus with evidence labels and a temporal split as a language resource for Sanskrit error detection and correction.

*Object of analysis: corrections/commits over dictionary source text — in scope per `docs/BOUNDARY_RULES.md`; the lexicographic-structure interpretation cross-links to `csl-atlas`.*
